UI/UX Engineer and Systems Programmer

Noble Roots was expected to be a farming/life simulation game with a focus on automating the farming process; developed using Unity engine and C# programming. You would start as a disgraced noble, and left with nothing but a small farm and be required to build your reputation with the townsfolk. Reputation would be tracked by a point system that incremented as you participated in several activities such as farming, communicating with the town-folk, and completing town quests. This game was a trial in what to do with failure. Every member of our team had issues with all different systems. I learned a lot working with Inventory systems and creating functional UIs during this project, and given the time, I probably could make these systems work as intended. .

My position for the game was UI/UX Engineer where my focus was to properly display informative elements of the game to the player, and the systems programmer with a primary focus on inventory systems and inventory management systems. During the first month of the game, I designed the base UI elements.

The Health system was fully functional but never implemented fully due to our gameplay programmer was not able to finish the combat system. However, we were able to finish implementing the stamina system, for testing purposes, the Player Character had a lot more than we planned to use; but we did get values working and changing based on the different actions the player takes. Tilling, Planting, and Watering crops all take stamina, and the meter will slowly drain. We intended for the player to pass out and wake up in their bed the next day if they ran out of stamina. However, we never solved the problem of saving the game which was necessary to stop the field from deleting its contents. Saving the game was one of the biggest issues during the last two months we were working on the game.

Turns out clocks are easy to make our sound designer finished it for me while I was designing the inventory UI. Unfortunately, since saving does not work the number for the day of the month increments but does not change anything but the number. Day 1 and 2... might as well be the same. Originally we wanted to use an analog clock, but none of us were certain how we would set it up so we kept the digital clock over an analog one.

This, and the inventory management system, was my baby. Did it work? Haha, no. This is the one thing I wanted to work on when starting the project. We all knew the inventory systems were going to be one of the more challenging tasks early in development. After developing the system for two months, I could see why systems like this usually have a team.
